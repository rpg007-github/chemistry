Temperature Conversions

K = °C + 273.15

°C = (°F - 32) × 5/9

Water freezes: 0°C = 273.15 K = 32°F

Water boils: 100°C = 373.15 K = 212°F

Absolute zero: 0 K = -273.15°C

Worked Example

Convert 98.6°F (body temperature) to Celsius and Kelvin.

Step 1: Use the Fahrenheit to Celsius formula: °C = (°F - 32) × 5/9
Step 2: °C = (98.6 - 32) × 5/9 = 66.6 × 5/9 = 37.0°C
Step 3: Convert to Kelvin: K = 37.0 + 273.15 = 310.2 K

Answer: 37.0°C = 310.2 K

Significant Figures

  • All nonzero digits are significant
  • Zeros between nonzero digits are significant (101 = 3 sig figs)
  • Leading zeros are NOT significant (0.0045 = 2 sig figs)
  • Trailing zeros after a decimal ARE significant (2.50 = 3 sig figs)
  • Multiply/divide: answer has fewest sig figs
  • Add/subtract: answer has fewest decimal places

Worked Example

How many sig figs in each: 0.00320, 4050, 100.0?

0.00320: Leading zeros don't count. The 3, 2, and trailing 0 after decimal are significant = 3 sig figs
4050: The zero is between/after nonzero digits with no decimal point. This is ambiguous, but typically = 3 sig figs (the trailing zero is uncertain without a decimal)
100.0: The decimal point makes all zeros significant = 4 sig figs

Classification of Matter

  • Pure substance: fixed composition. Either an element or a compound
  • Mixture: two+ substances physically combined. Homogeneous (uniform, like saltwater) or heterogeneous (non-uniform, like sand in water)
  • Physical change: no new substances (melting, boiling)
  • Chemical change: new substances formed (rusting, combustion)

Worked Example

A metal block has a mass of 57.3 g and a volume of 6.5 cm³. What is its density? Could it be iron (d = 7.87 g/cm³)?

Step 1: d = m / V = 57.3 g / 6.5 cm³
Step 2: d = 8.8 g/cm³ (2 sig figs, limited by 6.5)
Step 3: 8.8 g/cm³ is close to iron (7.87) but a bit high. It might be iron with measurement uncertainty, or a denser metal like nickel (8.91).

Answer: d = 8.8 g/cm³

Atomic Theory & Structure

Subatomic Particles

Properties of subatomic particles
ParticleChargeMass (amu)Location
Proton+1~1Nucleus
Neutron0~1Nucleus
Electron-1~0 (1/1836)Electron cloud
  • Atomic number (Z) = protons = defines the element
  • Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons
  • Isotopes: same element, different neutrons

Ions

  • Cation (+): lost electrons. Metals form cations.
  • Anion (-): gained electrons. Nonmetals form anions.
  • Charge = protons - electrons
"Cation" has a "t" like a plus sign (+)

Worked Example

An ion has 26 protons, 30 neutrons, and 23 electrons. What is the element, the mass number, and the charge?

Step 1: 26 protons = Iron (Fe), because atomic number defines the element.
Step 2: Mass number = protons + neutrons = 26 + 30 = 56
Step 3: Charge = protons - electrons = 26 - 23 = +3

Answer: Fe-56 with a 3+ charge, written Fe³⁺

Atomic Mass & Isotopes

  • Atomic mass on periodic table = weighted average of all naturally occurring isotopes
  • Avg mass = Σ(mass × fractional abundance)
  • 1 amu = 1/12 the mass of carbon-12

Worked Example

Chlorine has two isotopes: Cl-35 (mass 34.97 amu, 75.77% abundant) and Cl-37 (mass 36.97 amu, 24.23% abundant). Calculate the average atomic mass.

Step 1: Convert percentages to fractions: 75.77% = 0.7577, 24.23% = 0.2423
Step 2: Avg mass = (34.97 × 0.7577) + (36.97 × 0.2423)
Step 3: = 26.50 + 8.957 = 35.46 amu

Answer: 35.46 amu (matches the periodic table!)

Atomic Model History

  • Dalton: atoms are indivisible spheres
  • Thomson: "plum pudding" (discovered electron)
  • Rutherford: gold foil experiment, discovered small dense nucleus
  • Bohr: electrons in fixed energy levels
  • Quantum model: electrons in probability clouds (orbitals)

Electron Configuration & Periodicity

Quantum Numbers

  • n (principal): energy level (1, 2, 3...). Higher = farther, higher energy.
  • l (angular momentum): shape (0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f)
  • ml (magnetic): orientation (-l to +l)
  • ms (spin): +½ or -½

Filling Rules

  • Aufbau: fill lowest energy first
  • Pauli exclusion: max 2 e⁻ per orbital, opposite spins
  • Hund's rule: fill orbitals of equal energy singly first, then pair
Order: 1s 2s 2p 3s 3p 4s 3d 4p 5s 4d 5p 6s 4f 5d 6p

Electron Configuration

  • Full notation: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ (for Ar)
  • Noble gas shorthand: [Ne] 3s² 3p⁵ (for Cl)
  • Valence electrons = outermost shell electrons. Determine chemical behavior.
  • Group 1A = 1 val e⁻, Group 2A = 2, ..., Group 7A = 7, Group 8A = 8

Worked Example

Write the full and shorthand electron configuration for phosphorus (Z = 15). How many valence electrons does it have?

Step 1: 15 electrons to place. Follow the filling order: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p³
Step 2: Check: 2 + 2 + 6 + 2 + 3 = 15 ✓
Step 3: Noble gas shorthand: The nearest noble gas before P is Neon (Z = 10). So: [Ne] 3s² 3p³
Step 4: Valence electrons are in the highest shell (n = 3): 2 + 3 = 5 valence electrons. (Makes sense: P is in Group 5A!)

Answer: 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p³ or [Ne] 3s² 3p³, 5 valence electrons

Chemical Bonding

Bond Types

  • Ionic: electron transfer (metal + nonmetal). EN difference > 1.7. Crystal lattice, high melting point, conducts when dissolved.
  • Covalent: electron sharing (nonmetal + nonmetal). Polar if EN diff 0.4-1.7; nonpolar if < 0.4.
  • Metallic: "sea of electrons." Conducts, malleable, ductile.

Lewis Structures

  • Count total valence electrons
  • Least electronegative atom in center (H always terminal)
  • Connect with single bonds, complete octets on outer atoms first
  • If central atom needs more, make double/triple bonds
  • Octet rule: atoms want 8 valence e⁻ (H wants 2)
  • Exceptions: B/Be (incomplete), period 3+ (expanded)

Worked Example

Draw the Lewis structure for CO₂.

Step 1: Count valence electrons. C = 4, O = 6 each. Total = 4 + 6 + 6 = 16 e⁻
Step 2: C is less electronegative, so it goes in the center: O - C - O
Step 3: Connect with single bonds: O-C-O uses 4 electrons (2 per bond). Remaining = 12 e⁻
Step 4: Complete octets on outer atoms first. Each O gets 6 more e⁻ (3 lone pairs): uses 12. Done!
Step 5: Check C: it only has 4 electrons (2 bonds). Needs 8. Move lone pairs to make double bonds: O=C=O
Step 6: Final check: C has 8 e⁻ (two double bonds) ✓. Each O has 8 e⁻ (1 double bond + 2 lone pairs) ✓. Total = 16 ✓

Answer: O=C=O (each O has 2 lone pairs)

Lone pairs compress bond angles.

Worked Example

Predict the shape of NH₃ (ammonia).

Step 1: Draw Lewis structure. N has 5 valence e⁻. 3 H atoms contribute 3 e⁻. Total = 8 e⁻. N bonds to 3 H's and has 1 lone pair.
Step 2: Count electron groups around N: 3 bonds + 1 lone pair = 4 electron groups.
Step 3: 4 electron groups = tetrahedral arrangement. But with 3 bonds and 1 lone pair, the molecular shape is trigonal pyramidal.
Step 4: Bond angle is slightly less than 109.5° (about 107°) because the lone pair compresses the bonds.

Answer: Trigonal pyramidal, ~107°

Polarity & IMFs

  • Polar molecule = polar bonds + asymmetric shape (dipoles don't cancel)
  • Symmetric molecules can be nonpolar despite polar bonds (CO₂, CH₄)

Intermolecular forces (weakest to strongest):

  • London dispersion: all molecules. Stronger with more electrons.
  • Dipole-dipole: polar molecules
  • Hydrogen bonding: H bonded to F, O, or N

Stronger IMFs = higher boiling point

Nomenclature

Ionic Compounds

  • Metal + nonmetal: cation name + anion with -ide. NaCl = sodium chloride
  • Transition metals: use Roman numerals. FeCl₃ = iron(III) chloride

Worked Example

Name the compound CuSO₄ and write the formula for aluminum nitrate.

CuSO₄: SO₄²⁻ is sulfate (charge 2-). Cu must be 2+ to balance. Name: copper(II) sulfate.
Aluminum nitrate: Aluminum is Al³⁺ (always 3+). Nitrate is NO₃⁻. Need 3 nitrates to balance: Al(NO₃)₃

Answers: copper(II) sulfate; Al(NO₃)₃

Covalent Compounds

  • Nonmetal + nonmetal: use prefixes (mono, di, tri, tetra, penta, hexa)
  • No "mono" on first element. Second element gets -ide.
  • CO₂ = carbon dioxide. N₂O₅ = dinitrogen pentoxide

Acids

  • Binary acids (no O): hydro___ic acid. HCl = hydrochloric acid
  • From -ate ion: ___ic acid. HNO₃ = nitric acid
  • From -ite ion: ___ous acid. HNO₂ = nitrous acid

Worked Example

Name H₂SO₄ and H₂SO₃.

H₂SO₄: The polyatomic ion is SO₄²⁻ = sulfate (ends in -ate). Rule: -ate becomes -ic acid. Answer: sulfuric acid.
H₂SO₃: The polyatomic ion is SO₃²⁻ = sulfite (ends in -ite). Rule: -ite becomes -ous acid. Answer: sulfurous acid.

Answers: sulfuric acid; sulfurous acid

Stoichiometry

The Mole

  • Avogadro's number: 6.022 × 10²³ particles/mol
  • 1 mol of any element = its atomic mass in grams (molar mass)
  • moles = mass (g) / molar mass (g/mol)
  • # particles = moles × 6.022 × 10²³

Balancing & Reaction Types

  • Conservation of Mass: atoms in = atoms out. Adjust coefficients only.
  • Combination: A + B → AB
  • Decomposition: AB → A + B
  • Single replacement: A + BC → AC + B
  • Double replacement: AB + CD → AD + CB
  • Combustion: fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Stoichiometry Roadmap

grams A → moles A → moles B → grams B

  • Convert grams to moles (÷ molar mass)
  • Use mole ratio from balanced equation
  • Convert moles to grams (× molar mass)

Worked Example

How many grams of O₂ are needed to completely react with 10.0 g of hydrogen gas? The balanced equation is: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Step 1 (grams → moles): Molar mass of H₂ = 2.016 g/mol. Moles of H₂ = 10.0 g ÷ 2.016 g/mol = 4.96 mol H₂
Step 2 (mole ratio): From the equation: 2 mol H₂ requires 1 mol O₂. So 4.96 mol H₂ × (1 mol O₂ / 2 mol H₂) = 2.48 mol O₂
Step 3 (moles → grams): Molar mass of O₂ = 32.00 g/mol. Mass = 2.48 mol × 32.00 g/mol = 79.4 g O₂

Answer: 79.4 g O₂

Limiting Reagent & Yield

  • Limiting reagent: runs out first, determines product amount
  • Excess reagent: leftover
  • % yield = (actual / theoretical) × 100

Worked Example

For the reaction 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O, you have 5.0 mol H₂ and 2.0 mol O₂. Which is the limiting reagent? What is the theoretical yield of water?

Step 1: Calculate how much product each reactant can make.
From H₂: 5.0 mol H₂ × (2 mol H₂O / 2 mol H₂) = 5.0 mol H₂O
From O₂: 2.0 mol O₂ × (2 mol H₂O / 1 mol O₂) = 4.0 mol H₂O
Step 2: O₂ makes less product (4.0 vs 5.0), so O₂ is the limiting reagent.
Step 3: Theoretical yield = 4.0 mol H₂O × 18.02 g/mol = 72.1 g H₂O

Answer: O₂ is limiting; theoretical yield = 72.1 g H₂O

Empirical & Molecular Formulas

  • Empirical: simplest whole-number ratio (CH₂O)
  • Molecular: actual atoms (C₆H₁₂O₆)
  • Molecular = empirical × n, where n = molar mass / empirical mass
  • % composition = (mass of element in 1 mol / molar mass) × 100

Worked Example

A compound is 40.0% C, 6.7% H, and 53.3% O by mass. Its molar mass is 180 g/mol. Find the empirical and molecular formulas.

Step 1: Assume 100 g. That gives: 40.0 g C, 6.7 g H, 53.3 g O
Step 2: Convert to moles: C = 40.0/12.01 = 3.33 mol; H = 6.7/1.008 = 6.65 mol; O = 53.3/16.00 = 3.33 mol
Step 3: Divide by smallest (3.33): C = 1, H = 2, O = 1. Empirical formula = CH₂O
Step 4: Empirical mass = 12 + 2 + 16 = 30 g/mol. n = 180/30 = 6
Step 5: Molecular formula = C₆H₁₂O₆ (this is glucose!)

Answer: Empirical = CH₂O, Molecular = C₆H₁₂O₆

Gases

Gas Laws

Summary of gas laws
LawRelationshipFormula
Boyle'sP ↔ V (const T,n)P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Charles'sV ↔ T (const P,n)V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Gay-Lussac'sP ↔ T (const V,n)P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂
Avogadro'sV ↔ n (const T,P)V₁/n₁ = V₂/n₂
CombinedP, V, TP₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂

T must be in Kelvin for all gas law calculations!

Worked Example

A gas has a volume of 2.50 L at 25°C. What is its volume at 50°C if pressure stays constant?

Step 1: This is Charles's Law (V and T change, P constant): V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Step 2: Convert to Kelvin! T₁ = 25 + 273.15 = 298.15 K; T₂ = 50 + 273.15 = 323.15 K
Step 3: Solve for V₂: V₂ = V₁ × T₂/T₁ = 2.50 × 323.15/298.15 = 2.71 L

Answer: 2.71 L (volume increases because temperature increased)

Ideal Gas Law

PV = nRT

  • R = 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K
  • STP: 0°C (273.15 K), 1 atm
  • At STP: 1 mol gas = 22.4 L

Worked Example

What volume does 0.500 mol of nitrogen gas occupy at 2.00 atm and 27°C?

Step 1: List knowns: n = 0.500 mol, P = 2.00 atm, T = 27 + 273.15 = 300.15 K
Step 2: Rearrange PV = nRT to solve for V: V = nRT/P
Step 3: V = (0.500)(0.0821)(300.15) / (2.00) = 12.32 / 2.00 = 6.16 L

Answer: 6.16 L

Dalton's Law & Pressure

  • P_total = P₁ + P₂ + P₃ + ...
  • 1 atm = 760 mmHg = 760 torr = 101.325 kPa

Kinetic Molecular Theory

  • Gas particles in constant random motion
  • Volume of particles is negligible
  • No attractive forces between particles
  • Elastic collisions
  • KE proportional to temperature (K)
  • Lighter gases move faster at same T

Thermochemistry

Energy Basics

  • Exothermic: releases heat. ΔH < 0. Feels hot.
  • Endothermic: absorbs heat. ΔH > 0. Feels cold.
  • q_system = -q_surroundings

Heat Calculations

q = mcΔT

  • q = heat (J), m = mass (g), c = specific heat (J/g·°C), ΔT = T_final - T_initial
  • Specific heat of water: 4.184 J/g·°C

Worked Example

How much heat is needed to raise the temperature of 250.0 g of water from 20.0°C to 85.0°C?

Step 1: Identify values: m = 250.0 g, c = 4.184 J/g·°C, ΔT = 85.0 - 20.0 = 65.0°C
Step 2: q = mcΔT = (250.0)(4.184)(65.0)
Step 3: q = 67,990 J = 68.0 kJ (3 sig figs)

Answer: 68.0 kJ

Hess's Law & Enthalpy

  • Hess's Law: ΔH is the same regardless of path
  • ΔH°rxn = ΣΔH°f(products) - ΣΔH°f(reactants)
  • ΔH°f of any element in its standard state = 0

Worked Example

Calculate ΔH°rxn for: CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g). Given: ΔH°f of CH₄ = -74.8 kJ/mol, CO₂ = -393.5 kJ/mol, H₂O(g) = -241.8 kJ/mol

Step 1: ΔH°rxn = ΣΔH°f(products) - ΣΔH°f(reactants)
Step 2: Products: [1(-393.5) + 2(-241.8)] = -393.5 + (-483.6) = -877.1 kJ
Step 3: Reactants: [1(-74.8) + 2(0)] = -74.8 kJ (O₂ is an element in standard state, so ΔH°f = 0)
Step 4: ΔH°rxn = -877.1 - (-74.8) = -802.3 kJ

Answer: -802.3 kJ (negative = exothermic, which makes sense for combustion!)

Calorimetry

  • q_rxn = -q_water = -(mc ΔT) of water
  • Heat lost = heat gained (no heat loss to surroundings)

Worked Example

When a piece of metal (50.0 g, initially at 95.0°C) is dropped into 150.0 g of water at 22.0°C, the final temperature is 24.5°C. What is the specific heat of the metal?

Step 1: Heat lost by metal = heat gained by water. So: -q_metal = q_water
Step 2: q_water = (150.0)(4.184)(24.5 - 22.0) = (150.0)(4.184)(2.5) = 1,569 J
Step 3: For the metal: q = mcΔT, so 1,569 = (50.0)(c)(95.0 - 24.5) = (50.0)(c)(70.5)
Step 4: c = 1,569 / (50.0 × 70.5) = 1,569 / 3,525 = 0.445 J/g·°C

Answer: c = 0.445 J/g·°C (close to iron, c = 0.449)

Solutions

Molarity & Dilution

M = moles solute / liters solution

M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ (dilution)

  • Moles of solute stay the same during dilution
  • moles = M × V(L)

Worked Example

You dissolve 11.7 g of NaCl (molar mass = 58.44 g/mol) in enough water to make 500.0 mL of solution. What is the molarity?

Step 1: Find moles: 11.7 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.200 mol NaCl
Step 2: Convert volume: 500.0 mL = 0.5000 L
Step 3: M = 0.200 mol / 0.5000 L = 0.400 M

Answer: 0.400 M NaCl

Worked Example: Dilution

You need 250.0 mL of 0.100 M HCl. You have a stock solution of 6.00 M HCl. How much stock do you need?

Step 1: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂, where M₁ = 6.00 M, M₂ = 0.100 M, V₂ = 250.0 mL
Step 2: V₁ = M₂V₂/M₁ = (0.100)(250.0)/(6.00) = 4.17 mL
Step 3: Measure 4.17 mL of stock, then add water to bring total volume to 250.0 mL.

Answer: 4.17 mL of 6.00 M stock

Solubility & Electrolytes

  • "Like dissolves like": polar dissolves polar/ionic; nonpolar dissolves nonpolar
  • Strong electrolyte: dissociates completely (NaCl, HCl, NaOH)
  • Weak electrolyte: dissociates partially (CH₃COOH, NH₃)
  • Nonelectrolyte: no ions (sugar, ethanol)
